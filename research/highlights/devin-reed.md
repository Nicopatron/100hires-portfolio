## 1. Create content for the 95% who are NOT ready to buy

> "I spent a year at Gong creating content for the 95% of buyers who were NOT ready to buy. We grew from 12K to 220K LinkedIn followers and generated millions in pipeline — and never talked about our product. At Gong, I had one rule: No product talk. For an entire year. No features. No demos. No 'why Gong is better' posts. Just pure value for sellers trying to hit quota."

- **Source**: [Post 1, 2026-04-06](../linkedin-posts/devin-reed.md) (264 reactions)
- **Why it matters**: The most documented case of "95-5" content strategy in B2B SaaS. Disciplined enough to avoid product talk for 12 months straight. A Growth hire can point to this when defending "why aren't we posting about features?"

## 2. 74% of buyers evaluate 3-5 vendors simultaneously — you have to lead the list

> "74% of buyers evaluate 3-5 vendors simultaneously. So it's not enough to make the consideration list. You have to LEAD it. The only way to lead a list you can't see forming is to show up before it starts."

- **Source**: [Post 5, 2026-03-25](../linkedin-posts/devin-reed.md) (46 reactions)
- **Why it matters**: Converts Moroney's abstract argument into a buying behavior stat. The content operator's job isn't "be on the list" — it's "be at the top of the list." Changes the content bar from adequate to distinctive.

## 3. A mission statement that doesn't change — for years

> "My team had a mission statement that never changed the entire time I was there. 'Create the most engaging sales content on the f-ing planet.' It was the first slide in our strategy deck. Everyone on my team could recite it on command. Most teams change their mission every quarter. We kept ours for years."

- **Source**: [Post 7, 2026-03-18](../linkedin-posts/devin-reed.md) (365 reactions)
- **Why it matters**: Gives a Growth team the mandate to reject "should we also try TikTok?" pivots. Mission protection is the largest predictor of compounding output. 12-month content discipline > 12-quarter content drift.

## 4. Speak executive, not marketer

> "I used to walk into meetings with engagement metrics. Nobody cared. Now I start with: how content influences revenue, what's next (and why), invite questions and input. Save the click-through rates for the marketing team."

- **Source**: [Post 4, 2026-03-30](../linkedin-posts/devin-reed.md)
- **Why it matters**: The communication bar for a Growth hire working with a founder/CEO. Executive meetings need revenue-adjacent language; engagement metrics belong in the internal team review.

## 5. Newsletter sponsorship playbook

> "Lead with an educational offer, not your product. Newsletter readers are in learning mode. Ask for click-to-open rates, not just subscriber count. Know the subject line of your issue — it determines who opens. Open with curiosity, not a product description. I've seen 8x differences in performance based on the first two sentences alone."

- **Source**: [Post 2, 2026-04-10](../linkedin-posts/devin-reed.md) (146 reactions)
- **Why it matters**: Concrete paid-sponsorship tactical playbook. For 100Hires when they move into paid newsletter placements (HR Brew, Hung-Up, etc.), this is the due-diligence checklist before signing.

## 6. Show up before the list starts forming

> "95% of your buyers aren't in-market right now... But they will be. This is why investing in 'always on' brand plays like newsletters, podcasts, and events have huge upside. Because when the moment hits — when the tool breaks, when the budget opens, when the priority shifts — they reach for the name they already know. The B2B brands winning right now aren't waiting for buyers to raise their hands. They're the reason buyers think of them first when the hand goes up."

- **Source**: [Post 5, 2026-03-25](../linkedin-posts/devin-reed.md)
- **Why it matters**: The "always on" framing gives content a budget justification for a CFO. This is what you're paying for: not impressions now, but consideration-set placement when a trigger event hits months later.

## 7. Reject curated outcomes — study the conditions

> "You wake up, scroll LinkedIn, see a brand blowing up with millions of views and think 'we should do that!'... So you copy the play. And it doesn't work. Because you saw the curated outcome, not the conditions that created it. And visibility isn't proof. Following 'best practices' that ignore your situation is how you end up running someone else's play and still lose."

- **Source**: [Post 3, 2026-04-01](../linkedin-posts/devin-reed.md)
- **Why it matters**: Inoculation against copying any of the other 9 experts blindly. Each of these playbooks emerged from specific conditions. The Growth hire's job is to adapt, not port.
