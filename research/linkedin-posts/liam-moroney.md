## Post 1 — 2026-04-09

**Engagement**: 57 reactions | 29 comments | 5 shares

Marketers are supposed to understand the customer better than anyone, and it feels the opposite in tech these days.

I keep seeing variations of the same idea when it comes to AI:

"If AI can't find you, your buyer won't either"

"If you're not showing up in AI answers, you're invisible to buyers"

These are truly baseless claims and totally misunderstand how people buy complex consideration purchases.

The whole purpose of market research is to prevent us from making very expensive bets based on assumptions about audiences.

Like overestimating how real a problem is. Or overestimating how well we're known as a solution to that problem.

And incorrectly assuming how a product like ours is chosen and bought.

All of the buyer research to date in B2B has shown how much of buying comes from prior brand awareness and experience. How much is predetermined by day-one consideration sets that win 90% of the time.

How likely is it that all of that changed overnight, where buyers now cede all vendor choices to AI?

Yes, AI is being leveraged in most buying journeys now but that's fundamentally different to it being the foundation of who makes it into the consideration set.

That nuance matters, and makes the difference between how effective it will be to bet so much of the marketing strategy and effort on showing up in an AI answer.

If anything I think it makes the case for customer research more than ever, because we don't truly know how buying is changing through AI across segments.

And when we make dramatic claims like this we undermine our credibility and marketing.

## Post 2 — 2026-03-31

**Engagement**: 67 reactions | 17 comments

Somewhere along the way, we came to believe that marketing was the job of getting people to want the thing that you sell.

But it's always been about showing the market that what you sell gets them what they want.

And that means being sure that there is a problem there in the first place, in sufficient quantity to be able to build a business.

We have to be able to understand market demand in order to capture a share of it.

Of course, even with all the market research we can still be wrong, and even the best brands frequently are, but it's much worse to arrogantly believe in your power of persuasion to convince the market that there's a real problem to be solved.

And worse again to believe that you can accomplish that with hyper-efficient, predictable, and perfectly measurable marketing.

So many of the problems with modern marketing come from a belief that we and our offering is at the center of the universe — the marketing version of geocentric thinking.

But we are not the center, nor are we the hero in the story. It is always the customer, and it is always about their experience with the problem.

When we lose sight of that, we only become less effective.

## Post 3 — 2026-03-10

**Engagement**: 117 reactions | 12 comments | 4 shares

It's a pedantic but important point to say that the customer journey and the marketing journey are not the same thing, and the marketing journey is certainly not just a subset.

When we decide to buy something, there's a certain linearity to the effort, even if we stop and start along the way to making some kind of purchase decision.

You realize you have a problem, need, or desire, you evaluate options to solve or satisfy, and then you decide who to buy from — if anyone at all.

The time it all happens within can vary wildly, of course, since people and organizations come in and out of market throughout the journey, often pausing the effort as something else comes up that needs priority, and there's plenty of procrastination in between with different personality types.

So, even if often meandering and messy, it's still a journey from A to B, from problem to solution to decision.

The marketing journey, however, is not linear, and growth efforts can be tremendously harmed when it's made to be.

For most purchases, we don't only begin to learn about the brands once we've decided that we need to buy something. Instead, we pull from pre-existing awareness and memories where we can to form a starting point.

Anyone not in the memory bank before that customer journey starts is massively disadvantaged, since research continues to show that 80-90% of the time the deal will go to familiar brands who were day one considerations.

Marketing is non-linear because we don't know who's coming in market at any given moment, and so we need to plant as many of those brand memories in as many people as we can to improve the odds of being familiar to someone getting ready to buy.

Brand awareness is a state that we build and maintain as broadly as possible, so that when customer journeys begin they are pulling from a foundation of brand memories.

The healthier that foundation, the better our odds of selection, and the more effectively that 81% of the journey that involves engagement with the brand performs, and the more likely it moves onto sales engagement.

We love to talk about marketing and sales as a one-two combo, often like a relay team where marketing hands off MQLs that sales turns into SQLs, but that's only within the context of the customer journey.

The majority of the marketing journey is what's happening all the time around that, and where brand investments should be happening.

## Post 4 — 2026-03-05

**Engagement**: 41 reactions | 14 comments | 1 share

Brand gets underinvested in because it doesn't show up clearly enough in our attribution models, but should it?

Should attribution really be the central and core method for measuring marketing?

I'm not in any way anti-attribution, but I get incredibly frustrated at how much it's come to be how we, and especially leadership, believes that marketing works and gets measured.

What never gets measured in attribution is the underlying conditions that determine how well any of our efforts grow:

How many potential buyers know we exist, and are we continuing to create awareness in new buyers over time?

How effectively are we getting our brand to be thought of as an option to consider — something that becomes existential when purchase risk increases?

How successfully are we being thought of by those buyers when they actually do come in market?

None of this shows up in attribution tools, and yet those conditions determine just how much of an upper limit there is in the effectiveness of our demand gen and performance marketing efforts.

Brand is very possible to measure in a growing variety of ways, and has been for a long time, but it requires leadership understanding and belief in how it works.

When we force it to show up in a measurement tool designed for something else entirely, we set it up to fail.

It's like trying to measure climate change with a thermometer — it will very easily miss the bigger and more existential trends because it's too narrow a view.

Brand is measurable. It's the leadership understanding that's lacking.

## Post 5 — 2026-03-04

**Engagement**: 47 reactions | 13 comments | 1 share

When we apply a performance marketing mindset to the top of the funnel, we can end up putting a lot of effort and expense into building an audience that often isn't big enough, and sometimes isn't even real.

In B2B tech I'm increasingly seeing this happening, especially with the new obsession to rank in AI search results.

Where the effort is on trying to reach Goldilocks prospects who haven't been influenced by competitors yet, or even ones who haven't come to realize they need a solution yet, so we can come along and become their only solution.

While a very attractive concept, it's often an expensive effort to build an audience of unicorns.

The research in B2B continuously shows that most buyers are experienced ones, or at least part of their buying group is.

Blank slate buyers who are problem aware but not solution aware are a fringe group, if they even exist at all. Most of our addressable market knows and has experience with the category, and have some form of a consideration set in mind.

The job in marketing isn't to find the undiscovered buyers, but to win the underserved ones.

Our growth comes from people that exist in the big group in the middle, who have familiarity with the category and most of its players, have likely bought from at least one of them in the past, and don't need "educating".

They need a better alternative — and we have to show up often and relevantly to position ourselves in their consideration set.

## Post 6 — 2026-02-27

**Engagement**: 73 reactions | 13 comments | 4 shares

Word of mouth is not a tactic, but rather a byproduct of growth.

This is an important distinction, because we tend to look at things in marketing through a tactical and operational lens, where we can take steps to predictably and reliably increase things that work.

Word of mouth is not that.

The very reason that WOM is valuable is because it's an authentic reflection of your brand delivering value, enough that people will advocate for it to peers, followers, readers, etc.

And, when we look at research like the Wynter example, WOM is unsurprisingly much more effective at getting the brand into the consideration set.

But scaling it isn't as mechanical as the rest of the list, since it's not something we directly control.

For sure, we can make it easier for customers to reference and review us, we can create programs that incentivize it, but that incrementally improves things.

Word of mouth is ultimately tied to how many customers we have, how big our market share is, and how much awareness we have through brand marketing.

It's the reason to invest in brand, because you cannot be talked about if you are not known. It creates the conditions for WOM, and the conditions for all of the other tactics we have to better work.

Many of the outcomes from brand are indirect, and don't show up well in attribution tools, but they are also frequently the very thing that makes what does show up there work in the first place.

Word of mouth is very much an example of that.
