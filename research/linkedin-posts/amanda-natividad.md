## Post 1 — 2026-04-14

**Engagement**: 90 reactions | 53 comments | 2 shares

Your brand's public record is being written without you.

You may think you're shaping your brand through your website and your content. But increasingly, the internet is shaping it for you.

It's happening in AI answers, Reddit threads, reviews, comparison pages, and all the other places people form opinions before they ever visit your site. For better or for worse.

This is the part where I'm supposed to give you a horror story. But I'm an optimist, so let me flip the script.

Recently, I was building my Zero Click Marketing website and needed a tool to power my Contact page. I quickly became a Formspree customer because Claude recommended it to me. Claude even helped me write the code so I could just plug in my Formspree ID. The funny thing is, I became a customer without ever seeing any of Formspree's marketing, let alone its homepage. And if I had seen the homepage first, I probably wouldn't have converted — because they position themselves as a developer tool, and I am very much not a developer.

This doesn't mean we just surrender to whatever platforms, reviewers, communities, and AI systems happen to say about us. We still have a role to play in strengthening our own public record.

Here's how:
1) audit the current public record,
2) treat third-party surfaces like part of your brand footprint,
3) identify the weak claims that are winning,
4) publish better facts that only you can publish,
5) structure them so they're easy to retrieve and cite,
6) refresh the record continuously,
7) and measure whether the story the internet tells about you is getting better.

## Post 2 — 2026-04-07

**Engagement**: 73 reactions | 36 comments

Raise your hand if you've ever been personally victimized by a CRM.

Attribution tries to assign credit.
Measurement tries to understand impact.

And if you're only doing the first one, you're not really measuring your marketing. You're just watching the finish line.

Here's what Rand Fishkin's recent research makes painfully clear: search accounts for about 24% of visits to the top 5,000 websites. The other ~76% is where attention actually lives — social, news, email, forums, communities. That's where someone first hears your name, builds familiarity, and eventually decides to Google you.

Your attribution dashboard sees none of it.

Every time a CMO pulls budget from brand marketing to put more into Google Ads because "the numbers say search converts better," they're defunding the activities that made search work in the first place.

## Post 3 — 2026-04-02

**Engagement**: 567 reactions | 75 comments | 42 shares

Search isn't dead.

It's so alive that it now happens across Google, Amazon, YouTube, Reddit, AI tools, and all kinds of other places people go to look for answers.

Search is a behavior.

A few recent receipts:
- Rand Fishkin's research shows Google is still absurdly massive. In his analysis of the top 5,000 most-visited websites, Google was as large as the next 13 sites combined. It explains why search so often gets credit for demand that was created somewhere else first.
- Cyrus Shepard argues the job is not just to rank, or even just to win the click. It's to look like the result most likely to satisfy the searcher — and then actually do it.
- Ross Simmonds found that Reddit is showing up earlier in the buyer journey than many SaaS brands probably realize. In his research, buyers were often seeing Reddit discussions before vendor websites, even on general category searches.
- Wil Reynolds learned the hard way that if your strongest proof points aren't published, AI and search systems may piece together weaker third-party evidence to fill the gap.

## Post 4 — 2026-03-27

**Engagement**: 341 reactions | 75 comments | 6 shares

A friend asked me what's really driving my obsession with Zero Click Marketing.

Is it getting more likes?
Growing an audience?

Not really. It's because of a chip on my shoulder.

I didn't come up through traditional marketing training. I studied communications, worked in journalism, wanted to do food writing, and kind of stumbled into marketing. So I didn't enter this field thinking like a marketer first. I got here from having learned how to write & report, how to source things properly, and how to consume media with a critical eye.

Now... Zero Click Marketing doesn't feel like a new idea to me. It's a name for something I'd already been doing for years. It's about making things people actually want to consume. Things that are useful or interesting on their own. That don't need a click to justify their existence.

For a long time, I had to defend that way of thinking.

I'd worked with executives who looked at a draft and said, "It's fine. Just ship it."
Sometimes I'd say, "Okay, then let's put your name on it. Do you want to be associated with this?"

That usually changed the conversation. Because quality feels abstract right up until someone has to see their name right next to it.

I don't really care whether something can ship. I care whether it deserves to.

I really, truly think that Zero Click Marketing matters more than ever today.

Earn attention by being worth paying attention to.

## Post 7 — 2025-09-30

My most successful content marketing strategy? It had nothing to do with keyword research.

At Fitbit, when I helped grow the B2B business selling to employers, we:
- Published white papers that drove thousands of leads.
- Wrote case studies that closed deals.
- Planned conference agendas competitors outright copied (speakers and all!)

We weren't trying to rank for "what is corporate wellness?" Because no decision-maker worth selling to was Googling that.

Instead, we drove influence. By starting the conversations that decision makers actually want to have.

How? Audience research.

We learned what our buyers read, watched, and listened to. We spoke their language. We got on their level. All manually, through surveys, interviews, and 1:1 convos. It was a way of doing content marketing that no one was doing yet. Entirely audience and influence-driven.

## Post 8 — 2025-08-19

**Engagement**: 146 reactions | 46 comments

Audience research doesn't always start with interviews. Sometimes it starts with observation.

Here's a quick way to uncover insights in just a few minutes:
1) Pick 5 customers or prospects you already know.
2) Go to their LinkedIn. Read their headlines and About sections.
3) Copy/paste the exact words they use to describe themselves, their role, or their goals.
4) Compare across all 5. What words or phrases repeat?

Those phrases...
- Reveal how your audience self-identifies.
- Give you copy that will feel instantly familiar to them.
- And usually highlight problems or goals better than your internal personas.

Pay attention to your audience's language. There's a good chance they're articulating their problems better than you ever will.
