How Ramp is building an AI-pilled marketing team

A few months ago I met Drew Pinta (he supports marketing in a growth/data science at Ramp) on one of our webinars and thought he came across as an impressive guy. Useful and specific guidance, how to think about marketing strategy, and some key lessons they've learned at Ramp.

So I had him on for a 1:1 podcast and we talked about it:

- What it actually looks like to measure marketing when the hardest things to measure are often the ones working hardest
- Drew breaks down how Ramp uses incrementality testing and event studies to put real numbers on brand stunts, how they split budget 70/30 between proven channels and experimental bets, and how scanning thousands of Gong calls with AI surfaced attribution insights that contradicted every model they had.
- He also makes the bull case for why marketers might be better positioned than engineers in the age of AI (and doesn't shy away from the bear case either) but we also talked about the push inside of Ramp for AI adoption across the team.

Love this soundbite here: "MEASUREMENT SHOULD MEET THE MARKETING WHERE IT IS"

On my YouTube channel now and everywhere podcasts are these days.

## Post 6 — 2026-03-30

**Engagement**: 161 reactions | 52 comments

Two years ago after our first event - late night we joked "did we just become an events business?"

After dragging my heels on becoming an events business - because of the costs, the travel, the stress. It's one thing if people don't show up to your webinar or no one listens to your podcast, but to deal with people getting plane tickets, flying across the country, and getting their butts in seats for your event, man that is a different kind of stress.

But have ABSOLUTELY become an events business because in-person is an INCREDIBLE force multiplier for community.

Because we have this online community (Exit Five) some percentages of event attendees feel like they already know each other, and even if that's only 25% of the room it makes a big, big difference.

As Sydney Sloan put it:

"COMMUNITY EVENTS FEEL DIFFERENT. You can feel it the moment you walk in the room. Meeting people in real life for the first time after years of online connection feels fantastic. It's not networking — it's a reunion of people who actually care about each other. That doesn't happen at most customer events."

And the way I spin the story about IRL is that it's more important now, than ever, because we're all spending so much time working with AI, we can't forget about how great real human connection feels.

I've been told this is a very American thing, but we put a lot of stock into what we do for work. Our jobs are a big part of our identity, and there's something millions of people on this earth who work in the world of B2B marketing.

I'm honored that we've built something with Exit Five where we can sit at the middle of connecting those people.

I have LOTS of other event lessons and learnings, but I'm over the stage of sharing them now because we have something special and it's a secret sauce I'm keeping close.

Worth every penny of payroll going all in on this company after telling everyone I could only be solopreneur for life.

We have something special. "Who cares it's just B2B marketing bro." Nah you don't get it then.

PS. The NPS score from this event was an 89. Breaking our record of 88 from Drive 2024.

## Post 7 — 2026-03-30

Work with people who make things simple.

Smart people find simple solutions.

They don't try to sound smart.

They make things easy to understand.

They are not sending you in circles with numbers and metrics and dashboards and "business theater" that leaves you feeling more confused than when you started.

They make it simple, explain it simple, keep it simple. Almost always the case.

Dave Kellogg calls this persona the "Tasmanian Devil" inside the company and I thought that was the perfect framing.
