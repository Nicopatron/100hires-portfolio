## Post 2 — 2026-04-10

**Engagement**: 146 reactions | 23 comments | 2 shares

A CMO I advise who's struggling with distribution just asked me: "Is sponsoring newsletters really worth the money?"

I've been buying newsletter placements since my days running content at Gong and Clari. And I've sold and written 100+ ads for my own newsletter.

So I've seen what works and what burns budget fast.

My answer: They have huge upside, if you're set up to win.

What I tell every client before they sign the contract:

Lead with an educational offer, not your product.
Newsletter readers are in learning mode — they subscribed to get smarter, not to get sold. A benchmark report or guide will outperform a demo CTA almost every time.

Ask for click-to-open-rates, not just subscriber count. A tight list of 5,000 engaged readers beats a bloated list of 50,000. If they can't show you click-through rates, you're buying blind.

Know the subject line of your issue. It determines who opens — and who opens determines who clicks. Treat it like a targeting filter.

Open with curiosity, not a product description. I've seen 8x differences in performance based on the first two sentences alone. Lead with a claim, a story, or a finding — not "Acme is the leading platform for..."

The channel works. But only when the strategy, offer, and copy work together.

## Post 4 — 2026-03-30

**Engagement**: 66 reactions | 19 comments

When I became Head of Content, I learned the hardest part wasn't writing—it was working with the executive team.

After leading content at two $1B+ SaaS companies and advising dozens of founders, here's what I wish I knew sooner:

The real challenge? Bridging the gap between vision and execution. It wasn't about copy, campaigns, or conversion rates. It was about trust.

1. Turn "We need more content" into "Here's what actually works"
Instead of reacting to endless content requests, reframe the conversation.
- From "We need more leads" → Let's double down on what's converting best.
- From "We should be on every channel" → Here's where our audience actually spends time.
- From "We need to go viral" → Let's create something worth talking about—consistently.
Your job isn't just to produce—it's to set and protect the strategy.

2. Recognize the "Marketing vs. Sales" Tension
Sales wants pipeline now. But Marketing is playing the long game.
Your job? Balance both without caving to short-term thinking.
Push back when needed. But always have quick wins on the calendar.

3. Speak Executive (Not Marketer)
I used to walk into meetings with engagement metrics. Nobody cared.
Now I start with:
- How content influences revenue
- What's next (and why)
- Invite questions and input
Save the click-through rates for the marketing team.

4. Pick Your Battles (And Win the Right Ones)
Not every content request deserves a fight.
But some hills are worth fighting on—like positioning, messaging, and brand narrative.
Don't let "random acts of content" kill your strategy.

## Post 5 — 2026-03-25

**Engagement**: 46 reactions | 14 comments | 1 share

Influ2 just surveyed 50 business leaders on how software buying decisions actually happen. And the #1 answer caught me off guard.

The #1 way a buying journey starts is when a current tool stops meeting their needs (38%).

Not when a form gets filled out. Not when an account hits a scoring threshold.

But here's the problem with that…

By the time your ABM tool detects intent, that buyer has already started building a shortlist. They've consumed content, visited sites, and had internal conversations.

And here's where it gets tougher:
74% of buyers evaluate 3-5 vendors simultaneously.

So it's not enough to make the consideration list. You have to LEAD it.

The only way to lead a list you can't see forming is to show up before it starts.

95% of your buyers aren't in-market right now. They're not searching, not comparing, not ready.

But they will be.

This is why investing in "always on" brand plays like newsletters, podcasts, and events have huge upside. Because when the moment hits — when the tool breaks, when the budget opens, when the priority shifts — they reach for the name they already know.

The B2B brands winning right now aren't waiting for buyers to raise their hands.

They're the reason buyers think of them first when the hand goes up.
